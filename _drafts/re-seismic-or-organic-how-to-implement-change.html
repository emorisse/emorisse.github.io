Knowing when to throw your organization is into a seismic change is a hard question, and one with trade-offs.  Michael Pollock over at PollockSpark has some practical advice, and I began considering this question from what seismic changes do to relationship networks.

Delaying a seismic change has some clear benefits:

  1. You will learn more about about what isn't working today.
  2. You will lower the cost of the change itself, if you can take what you learn and be able to implement the change quicker.
  3. You have time to build a clear communications strategy.

You also have some messy drawbacks:

  1. Morale: large change is hard, but anticipation of large change is worse
  2. Increase in office politics: inevitable in any time of uncertainty
  3. Time to Market opportunity costs.  You're contemplating a seismic change because one of your fundamental assumptions has changed.  The sooner you can rebase to that new assumption, the sooner your organization can began working within it.

Your communication plan is important for any organization, but critical in any with a large number of knowledge workers.
