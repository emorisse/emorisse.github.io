Different types of customers, with different interests and needs, will buy your products. One way to group customers is when in the lifecycle of your product do they buy it? Do they purchase when a product has just been released? Do they purchase when it's been well proven by others?

If you group your customers together by when in the lifecycle of a new product they buy it, and put a line on top of how many of them there are, you have an adoption curve.

This is an adoption curve.

Everett Rogers' adoption curve is a model looking at when in the lifecycle of a product or technology, do individuals adopt. He groups customers into five categories:

  • Innovators, the first 2.5% of adopters.
  • Early Adopters, the next 13.5% of adopters.
  • Early Majority, the next 34% of adopters. (These first three are the first 1/2 of adopters)
  • Late Majority, the next 34% of adopters.
  • and Laggards, the last 16% of adopters.

Why does it matter? If you can only sell to one category of customer, your product will stall.

In Rogers' model, each of these five categories of customers have different risk tolerances, usually with supporting financial characteristics. For example, Innovators have a high tolerance for risk, and can afford to absorb losses if the outcome doesn't go well. Laggards often cannot easily absorb financial losses, and so chase lower risk product
